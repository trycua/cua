---
title: 'Fleet services'
description: "How declared sandbox services expose workload endpoints through Fleet's authenticated proxy."
---

Fleet services describe the endpoints that a sandbox exposes after it is claimed. A pool declares
services by name and target port as part of its template. Fleet uses those declarations to create
per-sandbox services, so the service identity is tied to both the sandbox and the declared service
name rather than to a pool-wide shared endpoint.

## A service is an interface contract

Names and labels identify which service belongs to which pool and sandbox. Ports connect the
Fleet-facing service to the port the process listens on inside the guest. Health paths and MCP
paths are then part of the application contract carried by the image. For example, an image may
serve a health endpoint such as `/healthz` and a Cua Driver MCP endpoint such as `/mcp`; those
paths are not implicit properties of every Fleet service.

A sandbox can expose more than one service. The primary workload endpoint can coexist with
auxiliary services for a desktop, a control plane, or application-specific dependencies. Declaring
them in the pool makes their names and ports part of the replica's compatibility definition, just
as the image and readiness rule are.

## Routing preserves the tenancy boundary

Clients reach a declared service through the authenticated route
`/api/svc/{namespace}/{service}/`. The proxy checks that the caller can access the namespace before
forwarding to the Kubernetes service, and it removes the caller's `Authorization` header before
the request reaches the guest. The namespace and service name are therefore both routing values and
authorization boundaries.

For a claimed sandbox, the service name includes the bound sandbox's identity together with the
declared service name. This is how a claim receives a private route to the endpoint associated with
its lease.

## Binding and readiness are separate states

A claim can be `Bound` before its guest's service is usable. This is common after a sandbox is
reset and rebooted for reuse: the allocation exists, while the guest is still starting its
processes. During that interval, proxy responses such as `502`, `503`, or `504` can be transient
signals that the upstream workload is unavailable rather than a change in claim ownership.

The important distinction is that a claim answers _which sandbox is leased_, while service
readiness answers _whether the workload endpoint can respond now_. Connection details belong in the
Fleet how-to guides; this page defines the boundary those tasks rely on.
