---
title: 'Fleet claims'
description: 'How an OSGymSandboxClaim leases one compatible Fleet sandbox to one workload.'
---

An `OSGymSandboxClaim` is a workload's request and lease for one sandbox from Fleet capacity. Its
`sandboxTemplateRef` identifies the compatible sandbox template that the claim needs. In the
current pool convention, a pool supplies a template named `<pool>-template`, and the claim refers
to that name. This directs the claim to the capacity maintained for that pool.

## A claim is a lifecycle, not just a lookup

A new claim is normally `Pending` while Fleet finds or prepares a compatible replica. It becomes
`Bound` when it has been assigned a sandbox, and a `Failed` phase records that the claim could not
be satisfied. `Bound` is an allocation result: it says which sandbox is leased, not that every
service inside the guest is already ready to answer requests.

The lease is exclusive. A bound sandbox belongs to that claim rather than being shared between
unrelated workloads. This preserves the meaning of an isolated computer: files, processes, UI
state, and service state accumulated during a lease are associated with one claimant.

## Release returns capacity, not guest state

Deleting a claim releases its lease. The released sandbox can return to the pool, but it must be
reset before reuse. The guest reboot that follows a reset means a sandbox can be bound to a new
claim before its in-guest services have completed startup. A caller that needs an HTTP or MCP
endpoint therefore treats claim binding and service readiness as separate conditions.

This reset boundary is why a claim is a better mental model than a permanent machine identifier.
The stable object for a workload is its lease; the underlying reusable sandbox is Fleet capacity
that may later serve another claim after it has been returned to a known state.

## Claims sit inside an authorization boundary

Creating claims requires per-user authorization in the current Fleet flow. Per-user credentials
carry the identity used for namespace access and can create claims; pool-scoped credentials do not
provide that Kubernetes identity. This protects the relationship between a claim, its pool
namespace, and the sandbox it receives.

Once a claim is bound, [services](/concepts/fleet/services) provide the route to the workload
running inside its sandbox.
